Deep Learning (AlphaFold3)

Although the structural analysis could have been performed using AlphaFold 2 by employing phosphomimetic mutations—such as the Y173D substitution used in in vivo experiments to demonstrate the impact of phosphorylation—this approach has inherent limitations. While aspartic acid partially mimics the negative charge of a phosphorylated tyrosine, it remains a surrogate that does not fully capture the specific steric and chemical properties of a true phosphate group. Consequently, AlphaFold 3 was selected for this study, as it has been specifically trained on datasets containing Post-Translational Modifications (PTMs). This allows for a more biophysically accurate representation of the monomeric state of MoOsm1, providing deeper insights into the precise structural hindrances and conformational changes triggered by actual phosphorylation at the Y173 position.

Consequently, both states were modeled. Analysis of these models reveals two key structural requirements that must be met. First, in the unphosphorylated monomer, the activation site must be solvent-accessible to allow the upstream kinase to perform the phosphorylation. Additionally, in the unphosphorylated dimer, the TGY motif is expected to be buried within the complex interface, specifically at the protein-protein interaction site. This configuration explains why phosphorylation at this position prevents dimerization, as the addition of bulky, negatively charged phosphate groups would physically and energetically disrupt the assembly of the dimeric interface.

Methods

Using the AlphaFold3 server, as previously detailed, structural modeling was performed for both the monomeric (copies: 1) and dimeric (copies: 2) states of the MoOsm1/MoHog1 protein. In all generated models, phosphorylation at Threonine 171 (T171) was maintained as a constant modification. Additionally, both the monomer and the dimer were modeled with and without the phosphorylation at Tyrosine 173 (Y173)(Figure 1). While the phosphorylated dimeric state does not occur naturally in vivo, it was explicitly modeled to analyze how this specific post-translational modification (PTM) affects the structural integrity and regulation of the complex.

This approach allows for a precise characterization of the conformational shifts and potential steric clashes induced by the addition of phosphate groups in the activation loop. Specifically, the phosphorylated dimer model was generated to examine the steric hindrance caused by the phosphate group at Y173, which is known to prevent stable dimerization. Conversely, the unphosphorylated monomer (at Y173) was modeled to establish the baseline localization of the TGY motif in its basal, yet T171-phosphorylated, state.

The structural consistency of the models generated by AF3 was evaluated by comparing the five independent ranked outputs for both the dual-phosphorylated monomer (pT171/pY173) and the phosphorylated dimer (pT171), using a Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) threshold of < 1 Å to define high-confidence convergence.

To analyze the MoOsm1 structural models, a standardized PyMOL workflow was implemented, following the same evaluation protocol and modeling confidence standards established for the study of Protein 1 (ORAI1) (?@sec-prot1-methods).

The TGY activation motif was specifically isolated for detailed inspection by creating a dedicated selection: select Osm1Mono_bothP_TGY, quarternarystate_PTMs and resi 171+173. To facilitate structural analysis, these residues were rendered as sticks with show sticks, Osm1Mono_bothP_TGY and highlighted in color red, Osm1Mono_bothP_TGY. Furthermore, the phosphate groups were emphasized by rendering phosphorus atoms as spheres with show spheres, (Osm1Mono_bothP_TGY and name P) and a scale adjustment of set sphere_scale, 0.3, (Osm1Mono_bothP_TGY and name P).

For publication-quality figure export, the following environment settings and high-resolution rendering commands were applied. The background was set to a neutral white using bg_color white, and shadows were disabled to enhance structural clarity with set ray_shadows, 0. Additionally, the visual representation of alpha-helices was refined through set cartoon_fancy_helices, 1. Finally, the images were generated using high-resolution ray-tracing with the command ray 1200, 1200.

Discussion

In the first place, the pTM and ipTM scores collectively indicate that while the global kinase fold is consistently maintained, the local and interfacial stability varies significantly depending on the phosphorylation state. The high reliability of the Thr171P/Thr173P monomer suggests that the simultaneous presence of both phosphate groups acts as a critical stabilizing factor for the activation loop, likely supported by the availability of dually phosphorylated homologous templates in experimental databases. In contrast, the lower pTM values for the monophosphorylated monomer and the dimeric models reflect a higher degree of localized structural uncertainty. The slightly higher pTM of the monophosphorylated dimer (0.58) compared to the diphosphorylated versions suggests that the accumulation of four phosphate groups in the dimer may introduce electrostatic or steric instability that the model cannot resolve as a rigid complex. Consequently, while phosphorylation clearly stabilizes the monomeric activation loop, these computational results do not provide evidence that double phosphorylation enhances the stability of the dimeric interface beyond previously reported experimental observations, suggesting instead a highly dynamic or transient interaction.

The alignment of the models of the dual-phosphorylated monomer (@fig_P3_Osm1Mono_bothP_AFcolored) indicates a highly robust and virtually identical conformational landscape across all iterations, suggesting a stable and well-defined fold for the activation loop in this state.

In contrast, the dimeric models (@fig_P3_dimer_171P_AFcolored) exhibited a significant structural deviation in the lower-ranked models. It suggests that, although the primary prediction for the dimer remains relatively stable, the overall modeling robustness is lower than that of the monomer. The 30 Å shift likely reflects a failure of the algorithm to consistently resolve the dimeric interface under the tension of the phosphate group, highlighting a higher degree of uncertainty or potential conformational instability in the dimeric assembly compared to the monomeric state.

Regarding the results obtained (Table 3), the ranking score of 0.89 for the monomer indicates very high confidence in the predicted structure. In contrast, the dimer shows a much lower ranking score (0.30), suggesting that the overall prediction is significantly less robust in the oligomeric state. The pTM value of 0.87 for the monomer further supports the reliability of the global fold. In the same line, the ipTM value of 0.22 for the dimer indicates weak confidence in the predicted interface between the two subunits, suggesting that the inter-chain interactions are not consistently resolved by the algorithm. It is also noteworthy that the fraction of disordered residues is very low in both cases (0.04 for the monomer and 0.03 for the dimer), indicating that most of the structure is predicted to adopt a stable folded conformation. In addition, no steric clashes were detected in either model, confirming that the atomic geometry of the structures is physically plausible.

I-Tasser

Justification of the method

I-TASSER was selected for its robust fragment assembly approach guided by threading techniques, a methodology that enables the prediction of protein structures even when close homologs are limited (@Zheng2021, @Zhang2017, @Yang2015). While more computationally intensive than AlphaFold, it offers significant advantages for enzymes like kinases due to its specialized modeling of active sites and functional domains. Furthermore, the integration of C-I-TASSER provides essential confidence scores and structural templates that validate the predicted models, making it a highly reliable tool for targets where precision in functional regions is critical

In the specific case of MoOsm1, although several experimentally resolved homologs exist, sequence identities generally hover around 40%. While this level is acceptable for classical homology modeling, a threading-based approach was employed to gain an independent structural perspective. By integrating fragment-based ab initio folding, I-TASSER can better resolve regions where evolutionary divergence might lead to local structural variations. Additionally, the detailed Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) provided during the threading process allows for verification of the conserved TGY motif across selected templates, ensuring the structural integrity of this critical functional domain in the final model.

Methods

The structural modeling of the protein was conducted using the I-TASSER suite under its default parameters, allowing for an unbiased exploration of the conformational space. The process integrated LOMETS meta-threading for template identification with iterative Monte Carlo simulations for fragment assembly. By adhering to the server’s standardized protocols, the final models were refined through FG-MD and REMO, ensuring a high-confidence structural ensemble validated by their respective C-scores and estimated TM-scores.

Results

The I-TASSER structural prediction for MoOsm1 yielded high-confidence results, particularly for the top two models. Model 2 exhibited the highest convergence (C-score = 0.44), closely followed by Model 1 (C-score = 0.35). The estimated TM-score of 0.76 ± 0.10 for the primary model confirms a high topological accuracy, significantly exceeding the 0.5 threshold for correct global folding. Given the sharp decline in confidence scores for Models 3 and 4, subsequent structural analysis was focused exclusively on the high-ranking models to ensure biological relevance.

The local reliability of I-TASSER structural models is represented by the ResQ (Residue-specific Quality) by providing a statistical estimation of the distance error (in Ångströms) for each residue relative to its native position(which is estimated based on model convergence, consistency with structural templates, and threading uncertainty). Therefore, low values indicate high precision and confidence, while high values signal regions of greater structural uncertainty. Given that the estimated local accuracy threshold in the ResQ plot (?@fig-pnorm-Bfactor-p3-1) is predominantly situated around 3 Å, this same value was established as the cutoff for the structural color-coding to ensure visual consistency between the quantitative graph and the 3D model.

Although ResQ and AlphaFold’s pLDDT are different metrics, a similar color scale has been implemented for visual consistency: marine (dark blue) represents the areas of highest reliability and orange indicates lower modeling quality (?@fig-pnorm-Bfactor-p3-2). To apply this representation in PyMOL, the following commands were used: color marine, Itasser_model1, color lightteal, Itasser_model1 and b > 3, color yellow, Itasser_model1 and b > 5.5, color orange, Itasser_model1 and b > 9, and color red, resi 171 to specifically highlight the position of interest.

The B-factor measures the inherent thermal mobility of residues, here reported as a normalized profile (\(B = (B' - \mu) / \sigma\)) to identify regions of relative flexibility or stability throughout the protein sequence (?@fig-pnorm-Bfactor-p3-3).

Modeller

Justification of the method

Due to the lack of an experimental structure for the target protein, the Modeller algorithm was selected for structural prediction.

Methods

To ensure the highest modeling accuracy, six high-quality templates were identified based on sequence similarity and high coverage, as detailed in Table 5. These templates correspond to X-ray diffraction-resolved structures from various organisms. To enhance alignment resolution and similarity detection, a specialized template search was conducted using two iterations of PSI-BLAST. The selection process strictly prioritized templates maintaining at least 50% Sequence Identity and a Query Coverage (QCoverage) exceeding 90%, ensuring a robust structural foundation for the homology modeling process.

Subsequently, the structural data for the six selected templates—3P5K, 3K3I, 8H59, 7W5C, 6RFP, 2B9F—were retrieved from the RCSB PDB database using the ‘Fetch by ID’ tool in ChimeraX. To ensure a clean structural environment for modeling, all non-standard residues, including ligands and water molecules, were identified and deleted (Actions > Atoms/Bonds > Delete). The target sequence, MoHog1, was imported from a .fasta file and integrated into the alignment using the ‘Best-aligning pair of chains’ protocol. Within the Multalign Viewer, a sequence realignment was executed to optimize the residues’ positioning. Every template structure was then manually associated with its corresponding sequence in the alignment, ensuring that the MoHog1 sequence remained unassociated (‘None’) as the target for homology.

For the modeling phase, all six structures were selected as templates. The homology modeling was performed via the Modeller (homology) interface. Advanced configuration parameters were adjusted to define the total number of models to be generated, ensuring a robust sampling of the conformational space for the final MoHog1 structure.

The structural integrity of the best-ranking MoHog1 model was validated using the zDOPE (Discrete Optimized Protein Energy) score. The model achieved a zDOPE value of -1.45, which is significantly below the -1.0 threshold typically used to identify high-quality, native-like folds. This score confirms that the spatial arrangement of residues is energetically favorable and statistically consistent with experimental protein structures, further supporting the reliability of the homology modeling process.

Results

The structural integrity of the unphosphorylated MoHog1 monomer was validated through a comprehensive alignment against the six experimental templates used in the homology modeling process. The structural convergence was highly consistent across all templates, with Root Mean Square Deviation (RMSD) values ranging from 0.759 Å to 1.113 Å. Specifically, the closest structural proximity was observed with template 6RFP (0.759 Å over 1,743 atoms), while the maximum deviation was found with 3P5K (1.113 Å over 1,910 atoms). These low RMSD values, calculated over a significant fraction of the 2,897 total atoms in the predicted monomer, demonstrate a high degree of conformational conservation and confirm that the modeled kinase core strictly maintains the canonical architecture observed in homologous X-ray structures.

The structure is color-coded by transferred B-factors, ranging from cool tones (marine, B \(\approx\) 25) to warm tones (orange, B \(\ge\) 100).
